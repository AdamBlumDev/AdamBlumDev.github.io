Everything About the Web is Wrong

The World Wide Web has aban­doned its dreams of be­ing a global de­cen­tral­ized in­for­ma­tion net­work, in fa­vor of ad­ver­tise­ment and pro­pa­ganda. What prob­lems could be solved in or­der for us to make the Web a more pleas­ant place?

Unintuitive Paradigms

We rely on the archetypes cre­ated by pre­vi­ous gen­er­ations of com­puter sci­en­tists and soft­ware de­vel­op­ers. In hind­sight, maybe some seem­ingly ar­bi­trary de­ci­sions have kept us from a more pleas­ant ex­pe­ri­ence.1

For ex­am­ple, be­cause the na­ture of a web­page is to ex­tend ver­ti­cally, we end up with a long, con­tin­uous column of text. A more nat­ural paradigm might be a fixed height (the height of your screen), but with hor­izon­tal scrolling. Not only does this more closely re­sem­ble a book, but it means CSS columns ac­tu­ally have a sen­si­ble place in our stylesheets. By de­fault, ev­ery web browser’s stylesheet stretches para­graphs to one or two lines of eye-strain­ing length.

Tim Bern­ers-Lee isn’t an ex­pert in ty­pog­ra­phy, he is a com­puter sci­en­tist; no­body could rea­son­ably ex­pect a beau­ti­fully-type­set Web from the be­gin­ning. That be­ing said, it’s been decades since the Web was born, and im­prove­ments on the CSS and HTML stan­dards seem fo­cused more on style than sub­stance. We don’t need more Javascript frame­works that an­imate and in­finite-scroll; not only are they pro­cess­ing-in­ten­sive, they gen­er­ally serve to bring more at­ten­tion to ad­ver­tise­ments and other use­less dig­ital junk food. Once we can write a de­cent-look­ing es­say on the Web, per­haps these bits of eye-candy can be re­vis­ited.

Understanding the Medium

There are also a lot of prob­lems in the gen­eral de­sign com­mu­nity when it comes to de­sign­ing for the web.

  • Ev­ery­one thinks “ty­pog­ra­phy” means enor­mous head­ers and body copy larger than 14px, while des­ecrat­ing the page with ad­ver­tise­ments and so­cial me­dia wid­gets.

  • CSS columns can’t be au­to­mat­ically bro­ken by page height due to the flex­ible na­ture of the web. Their only real use is in print stylesheets
