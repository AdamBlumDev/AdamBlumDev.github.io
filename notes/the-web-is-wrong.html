The World Wide Web has aban­doned its dreams of being a global de­cen­tral­ized in­for­ma­tion net­work, in favor of ad­ver­tise­ment and pro­pa­ganda. This rev­olu­tionary tech­nology has been stripped of its dig­nity.

Of Course, It’s Capitalism

The W3C sets the stan­dards for the Web. One look at their corporate donors tells you ev­ery­thing you need to know. There are a few well-meaning or­ga­ni­za­tions, like the Wiki­media Foun­da­tion, the Apache Soft­ware Foun­da­tion, the Na­tional Fed­er­ation of the Blind, etc. Then there’s the usual shitty sus­pects: Amazon, Adobe, Net­flix, Amer­ican Ex­press, Dis­cover, Visa, Mas­ter­card, PayPal, Disney, AT&T, Com­cast, Boeing, Face­book, and many more. I don’t want these people any­where near Web stan­dards. They seek to mon­etize every as­pect of our lives; the con­cept of “free” is only useful when it can be used to lure you into ac­tu­ally buying some­thing.

They have been accepting such sponsors since 2011, some donating over $100,000 each year. How can you make your voice heard against that kind of money? You can’t.

Unintuitive Paradigms

We rely on the archetypes cre­ated by pre­vious gen­er­ations of com­puter sci­en­tists and soft­ware de­vel­opers. In hind­sight, maybe some seem­ingly ar­bi­trary de­ci­sions have kept us from a more pleasant ex­pe­ri­ence.1

For ex­ample, be­cause the na­ture of a web­page is to ex­tend ver­ti­cally, we end up with a long, con­tin­uous column of text. A more nat­ural paradigm might be a fixed height (the height of your screen), but with hor­izontal scrolling. Not only does this more closely re­semble a book, but it means CSS columns ac­tu­ally have a sen­sible place in our stylesheets. By de­fault, every web browser’s stylesheet stretches para­graphs to one or two lines of eye-straining length.

Tim Berners-Lee isn’t an ex­pert in ty­pog­raphy, he is a com­puter sci­en­tist; no­body could rea­son­ably ex­pect a beau­ti­fully-typeset Web from the be­gin­ning. That being said, it’s been decades since the Web was born, and im­prove­ments on the CSS and HTML stan­dards seem fo­cused more on style than sub­stance. We don’t need more Javascript frame­works that an­imate and in­finite-scroll; not only are they pro­cessing-in­ten­sive, they gen­er­ally serve to bring more at­ten­tion to ad­ver­tise­ments and other use­less dig­ital junk food. Once we can write a de­cent-looking essay on the Web, per­haps these bits of eye-candy can be re­vis­ited.

Understanding the Medium

There are also a lot of prob­lems in the gen­eral de­sign com­mu­nity when it comes to de­signing for the web.

  • Ev­eryone thinks “ty­pog­raphy” means enor­mous headers and body copy larger than 14px, while des­ecrating the page with ad­ver­tise­ments and so­cial media wid­gets.

  • CSS columns can’t be au­to­mat­ically broken by page height due to the flex­ible na­ture of the web. Their only real use is in print stylesheets
