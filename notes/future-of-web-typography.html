The Future of Web Typography

  • Punctuation
  • Strange Ideas

As it cur­rently stands, quality ty­pog­raphy on the Web is nearly im­pos­sible to achieve. As stated in a previous article, the In­ternet now re­sides within the tight fists of cor­po­ra­tions, who are happy to show you pic­tures and videos all day. We are seeing some good ad­di­tions coming in the fu­ture, how­ever:

Punctuation

Hy­phen­ation through CSS1 is finally in the works, and it can’t come soon enough. The rag of an average paragraph on the Web is distractingly uneven. They are the foundation of our text content, and yet they’re unacceptable. The only current cross-browser solution, in­serting soft hy­phens, cre­ates more prob­lems than it solves: they ruin ac­ces­si­bility, per­sist after copy-pasting, and add sig­nif­icant weight to text-heavy pages.

Hanging punc­tu­ation2 will fur­ther sculpt the newly-minted text blocks, by pulling punc­tu­ation to the mar­gins.

Counter Styles3 will allow one to de­fine custom coun­ters for list items (① ② ③, “First” “Second” “Third”), ref­er­ence marks (*, †, ‡, §, ‖, ¶), and other in­cre­menting el­ements.

Vari­able Fonts don’t seem ap­pealing to me. I saw no need for con­tin­uously vari­able font weights, and few of the demos4 did any more than that. Matthew But­terick has a very de­tailed ar­ticle5 ex­plaining just how use­less they are. How­ever, using vari­able fonts for op­tical sizing could be an in­ter­esting idea, as­suming type de­signers could de­sign them ef­fec­tively.

Strange Ideas

When it comes to citing sources, the <cite> el­ement is about all we have at our dis­posal. It’s strange to me that there is no way to ref­er­ence other media via an ex­ternal file à la BibTeX6. Imagine being able to load a ref­er­ence file via <meta> tag, with CSS-like def­ini­tions:

@book reference_name {
    title: "The Book Title";
    author: "John Smith";
    publisher: "Publisher";
    year: "2004";
}

It could then be used in HTML to generate a formatted citation. This:

<ref value="reference_name"/>

Could be ren­dered in the DOM as a <cite> el­ement, or maybe some­thing en­tirely dif­ferent.
